§ 1 — Introduction

Smart Parking
Allocation Algorithm

A congestion-sensitive mathematical model for balanced parking distribution

In conventional parking systems, arriving vehicles are directed to the nearest available spot. This simple rule minimises individual walking distance but systematically overloads zones near the entrance while remote zones remain underutilised — a classic tension between efficiency and fairness.

This project introduces a cost-function-based allocation model. Each candidate spot is scored by a weighted combination of travel distance and zone congestion, and the vehicle is assigned to the minimum-cost spot.

Cost Function C = d + α · L
SymbolMeaning
CTotal cost of assigning a vehicle to a candidate spot
dManhattan distance from the parking lot entry to the spot
LCurrent load of the zone containing the spot (number of cars parked)
αCongestion sensitivity — controls the efficiency–fairness trade-off

By varying α, the system's behaviour shifts continuously. When α is small the model behaves like a nearest-spot algorithm; when α is large it distributes vehicles evenly across zones regardless of distance.

Performance Evaluation

After simulation, system performance is measured by three quantities. Total travel distance D = ∑di captures overall efficiency. Zone imbalance I = |L1 − L2| captures fairness. Both are normalised to [0,1] and summed into a combined score Score = Dnorm + Inorm, where a lower score is better. Details and graphs are in §5.

§ 2 — Mathematical Model

Variable Definitions

The parking lot consists of 5 rows × 10 columns (50 spots total). Spots are divided into two equal zones at the column midpoint: Zone 1 covers columns 0–4, Zone 2 covers columns 5–9. The entry point is at the top-left corner.

Distance is computed using the Manhattan metric, which reflects movement along row and column grid paths:

Distance Metric d(x,y) = |x1 − x2| + |y1 − y2|

The zone load L for each zone increments by 1 each time a vehicle parks there. As L grows, that zone becomes less attractive in the cost function — naturally redistributing later arrivals toward the emptier zone.

Efficiency–Fairness Trade-off

The parameter α encodes a policy decision: how much extra travel distance is acceptable in order to achieve balanced zone usage?

Efficiency
Fairness
Small α — Efficiency Priority
α → 0
Cost ≈ d   (distance only)

Zone load term is suppressed. The algorithm always selects the nearest available spot. Congestion builds near the entry; Zone 1 fills rapidly while Zone 2 stays underused.

Large α — Fairness Priority
α → large
Cost ≈ α·L   (load dominates)

Zone load dominates. Cars are guided into the emptier zone even if it means a longer walk. Both zones fill evenly and entry-side congestion is avoided.

§ 3 — Algorithm

Allocation Procedure

The following steps are executed for each arriving car. The procedure is greedy — each vehicle independently selects the best spot available at the moment of arrival, with no lookahead or reservation.

1
Initialise the System
Before any cars arrive, set both zone load counters to zero and mark all 50 spots as empty.
zone_load[1] = 0,   zone_load[2] = 0
occupied = { }
2
A Car Arrives at the Entry Point
Cars arrive one at a time at the entry point (0, 0). The algorithm now evaluates every available spot to find the best assignment.
3
Compute the Cost for Every Available Spot
For each unoccupied spot, calculate its cost using C = d + α · L, where d is the Manhattan distance from entry to that spot and L is the current load of the zone that spot belongs to.
4
Select the Spot with the Lowest Cost
Compare all computed costs and choose the spot with the minimum C. This is the optimal assignment under the current α setting.
5
Park the Car and Update All Records
Mark the chosen spot as occupied, increment that zone's load counter, and add the travel distance to the running total.
occupied.add(chosen_spot)
zone_load[zone(chosen_spot)] += 1
total_distance += d
6
Repeat for the Next Arriving Car
Return to step 2. The updated zone loads now influence costs for subsequent cars — congested zones become less attractive automatically, creating self-regulating redistribution.
7
Record Final Performance Metrics
After all n cars have been allocated, record D and I for this α value.
D = total_distance
I = |zone_load[1] − zone_load[2]|

§ 6 — Key Insight

Interpretation

The simulation reveals a clear and measurable trade-off between efficiency and fairness as α is varied. Each regime produces a distinct system behaviour:

Low α
Efficiency Maximised
The cost function reduces to distance alone. Every arriving car parks in the nearest available spot. Travel distances are minimised but Zone 1 fills rapidly while Zone 2 stays largely empty.
D ↓ low I ↑ high Score: mixed
Medium α
Optimal Balance
The zone load penalty begins to redirect cars before one zone becomes heavily overloaded. Both distance and imbalance are kept at reasonable levels. The combined score is typically minimised here.
D ≈ moderate I ≈ moderate Score: ↓ minimum
High α
Fairness Maximised
Zone load dominates the cost function. Cars are aggressively redirected to the emptier zone regardless of distance. Zone imbalance drops near zero, but total travel distance increases noticeably.
D ↑ high I ↓ low Score: mixed
Mathematical Conclusion

The cost function C = d + αL is a weighted linear combination of distance and zone load. The parameter α acts as a continuous dial between two objectives: small α minimises individual travel distance; large α enforces equal zone usage. The combined score Score = Dnorm + Inorm quantifies both simultaneously, and the α that minimises it is the optimal trade-off point for a given configuration — neither extreme policy achieves the best overall outcome.

Future Scope: more than two zones; cars departing so zone loads decrease over time; α adjusting dynamically as the lot fills; multiple entry points; or probabilistic arrival patterns. Each addition increases realism while preserving the same cost-function structure.
